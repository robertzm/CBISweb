INTRODUCTION

CBIS activities are supported by the Department of Biostatistics and Bioinformatics at Emory University and external grants. Listed below are several grants that have contributed to CBIS research efforts.

Some grants achievements of CBIS members and collaborators can also be found in our CBIS Five-Year Report.

FUNDINGS

Statistical ICA Methods for Analysis and Integration of Multi-dimensional Data

Funded by: National Institutes of Health (NIMH), R01
Principal Investigator: Ying Guo, PhD. and Jian Kang, Ph.D.
Funding Period: 09/25/2014-07/31/2018

This project seeks to develop novel statistical independent component analysis (ICA) methods for integrating multi-dimensional data (multimodal imaging/genetics/ behavior) to enhance understanding of mechanisms and treatment response of mental disorders.

Method Development of Agreement Measures and Applications in Mental Health

Funded by: National Institutes of Health (NIMH), R01
Principal Investigator: Ying Guo, PhD., Amita K. Manatunga, Ph.D., and Limin Peng
Funding Period: 09/01/2013-08/3/2018

This proposal aims to develop new statistical methods to investigate the alignment between traditional behavior/clinical outcomes and neuroimaging biomarkers and also to assess agreement and calibrate images from multi-center neuroimaging studies.

Analytic Methods for Determining Multimodal Biomarkers for Parkinson's Disease

Funded by: National Institutes of Health (NINDS), U18
Principal Investigator: F. DuBois Bowman, Ph. D.
Funding Period: 10/2012-9/2015

This project is funded as a part of the NINDS Parkinson's Disease Biomarker Program. There has been considerable progress in understanding the biology of Parkinson's disease (PD). Reliable biomarkers are still lacking, however, for early stage detection of PD and for characterizing disease progression. We are pursuing two avenues that may reveal early-stage PD biomarkers. First, we are combining datasets from different imaging modalities [including neuromelanin magnetic resonance imaging (NM-MRI) of the locus coeruleus and the substantia nigra, chemical shift imaging (CSI), diffusion tensor imaging (DTI), and resting-state functional MRI], cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) analytes, genotype information, and numerous clinical variables and developing new statistical methods to identify multimodal PD biomarkers from these massive datasets. Secondly, we will consider a Kaiser Permanente clinical database with nearly 250,000 subscribers in Georgia and attempt to determine prior diagnoses, lab results, and medication histories that are risk factors for the subsequent development of PD.

Neuroimaging Correlates of Impaired Fear Inhibition in PTSD

Funded by: National Institutes of Health (NIMH), R21
Principal Investigator: Tanja Jovanovic, Ph. D.
Funding Period: 9/19/2012-5/31/2014

This study will use structural and functional MRI analyses to examine differences in PTSD related to impaired fear inhibition.

Anti-18F-FACBC PET-CT for the Detection and Staging of Recurrent Prostate Carcinoma

Funded by: National Institutes of Health (NCI), R01
Principle Investigator: David Schuster, M.D.
Funding Period: 2011-2012

The general objective of this research is to determine if PET imaging with anti-[18F]FACBC will lead to improved patient care in the diagnosis and staging of prostate cancer and to elucidate the mechanism of its uptake within malignant cells. The specific hypothesis behind this project is that anti-[18F]FACBC PET-CT will detect more local and extraprostatic recurrence than standard conventional imaging, especially compared with Indium-capromab-pendetide (ProstaScint).

Analysis of Ultra High-Dimensional Data in Imaging Genetics Studies

Funded by: Emory University Research Committee (URC) and the Atlanta Clinical and Translational Science Institute (ACTSI)
Principle Investigator: Jian Kang, Ph.D.
Funding Period: 2011-2012

The objective of this grant is to develop new statistical methods for the analysis of Ultra High-Dimensional Data in Imaging Genetics Studies.

Biostatistics in Genetics, Immunology, and Neuroimaging (BGIN)

Funded by: National Institutes of Health (NIGMS), T32
Principal Investigator: Lance Waller, Ph. D.
Funding Period: 7/4/2005-6/30/2013

The purpose of this grant is to provide Ph.D. students in Biostatistics the opportunity to learn to develop statistical and computational innovations applied in one of three areas of concentration, namely genetics, immunology, or neuroimaging. The Neuroimaging Track offers training in neuroscience and neuroimaging as well as a range of developmental activities such as laboratory rotations and participation in seminars.

Analytic Methods for Functional Neuroimaging Data

Funded by: National Institutes of Health (NIMH), R01
Principal Investigator: F. DuBois Bowman, Ph. D.
Funding Period: 7/15/2007-6/30/2011

The purpose of this grant is to develop novel statistical methods for 1) predicting patterns of distributed neural processing following a treatment intervention, 2) determing the likelihood of response to treatment on an individual basis, using neural processing information, and 3) developing an improved spatio-temporal modeling framework for analyzing functional neuroimaging data to identify prominent functional connections in brain activity and to identify task-related increases (decreases) in brain activity.

Statistical methods for group independent component analysis for multi-subject functional magnetic resonance imaging data

Funded by: Emory University Research Committee
Principle Investigator: Ying Guo, Ph.D.
Funding Period: 06/30/2009-06/30/2010

The objective of this grant is to develop new statistical methods for group independent component analysis (ICA) to estimate subject-specific spatial source signals and to establish formal statistical testing framework for between-group comparisons in spatial domain.

Statistical Methods for Neuroimaging Data

National Institutes of Health (NIMH), K25
Principal Investigator: F. DuBois Bowman, Ph. D.
Funding Period: 9/10/2002-8/31/2007

This project focuses on the development of new statistical methodology to analyze functional neuroimaging data, including both positron emission tomography (PET) and functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI). Specific goals focus on 1) the development, application, and evaluation of clustering methods to determine brain regions that exhibit similarities in task-related brain function, 2) the modeling of design-related correlations from repeated scanning sessions, and 3) spatial modeling of correlations between localized brain activity measurements.

Cocaine Dependence and Cognitive Control of Behavior

Funded by: National Institutes of Health (NIDA), R01
Principal Investigator: Clinton Kilts, Ph. D.
Funding Period: 8/15/2003-4/30/2007

The goal of this project is to isolate and compare the neural correlates of response inhibition in cocaine dependence and Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder to provide a functional neuroanatomical basis of comparison across disorders of impulse control.

Detecting Changes in Myocardial Perfusion and Function

Funded by: National Institutes of Health (NHLBI), R01
Principal Investigator: Tracy Faber, Ph. D.
Funding Period: 7/15/2002-6/30/2007

The objective of this project is to develop and validate computer-based methods to visualize serial changes in myocardial perfusion and function from single photon emission computed tomography. Furthermore, this study proposes new statistical methodology to quantify and test the significance of perfusion changes using models that account for temporal and spatial correlation among the multiple perfusion measurements of the left ventricle.

Wavelet Image Interpolation (WII): A Wavelet-Based Approach to Enhancement of Digital Mammography Images

Funded by: Georgia Cancer Coalition
al Investigator: F. DuBois Bowman, Ph. D. and Brani Vidakovic, Ph. D.
Funding Period: 3/1/2005-12/31/2006

The main objective of this research is to improve diagnostic capabilities of digital mammography images by developing a wavelet-based image enhancement procedure. The procedure refines the appearances of tiny, possibly malignant, microcalcifications that appear sparsely throughout digital mammograms.
